# Why Europe Grew Rich and Asia Did Not

## Chapter 1: Introduction
*   **Page 2**: Competition with Indian textiles led to mechanized production; lack of wood led to coal in 19th century United Kingdom.
*   **Page 4**: Classic story of European superiority includes: property rights, fertility checks, rationality.
    *   Most advanced parts of Asia had these as well!
*   **Page 9**: Book's argument:
    1.  Variety of economic/political goals in 17th-18th century led to multiple development paths.
    2.  Different results from human agency.
    3.  State actions were a "critical driver of change".
*   **Page 10**: No conscious attempt to create industrialization anywhere - it was an emergent phenomenon. United Kingdom.
*   **Page 13**: Key elements: United Kingdom = deforestation, global competition, state policy.

## Chapter 2: India and the Global Economy 1600-1800
*   **Page 22**: Polycentric world trade system in 17th, 18th century. No single center.
*   **Page 27**: Enormous global demand for Indian cloth on all known continents.
*   **Page 35**: Indian cloth generally more expensive but sold well due to very high quality.
*   **Page 39**: Wages in Bengal were lower than in Britain, cotton prices were lower in India, India had abundant raw material.
*   **Page 50**: Huge silver imports led to Indian economy being highly commercialized and monetized.

## Chapter 3: Political Institutions & Economic Life
*   **Page 53**: Long history of European views of static, despotic, irrational "Orient".
*   **Page 56**: Negotiated power, political competition, markets all existed in "Orient".
*   **Page 60**: Indian economy more monetized than Britain in late 18th century, better capital markets.
*   **Page 70**: Richest parts of India had big non-agricultural sectors and exported cloth and food.
*   **Page 74**: Similar family sizes etc. to Europe and China. India - no significant Malthusian differences.
*   **Page 82**: Richer record keeping / account of commerce in India, China. This suggests state rationalization.

## Chapter 4: The European Response to Indian Cottons
*   **Page 92**: European fabric printers preferred printed Indian because of ease of dyes.
*   **Page 98**: Key innovations (jenny, water frame) were a response to Indian competition.
*   **Page 102**: Most histories focus on push/pull between United Kingdom spinners and weavers with no reference to India.
*   **Page 113**: True driver of innovation = competition with Indian goods on basis of quality.

Note: maybe just luck that protectionism shielded a rapidly "advancing industry" against an established but technologically stagnant competitor?

## Chapter 6: From Cotton to Coal
152- 1770s-90s-1820s: steady rise in exports of United Kingdom yarn/cloth, re-ordering of global textile market.
153- Increase in cloth manufacturing = secondary trades supply machine parts etcetera = increase in technology/knowledge.
158- Eurasia-wide wood shortage in late 18th century. Increase in use of coal in Europe and China.
163- British state intervened to promote coal industry (170). China did for less because no need and focused on food.
176- Japan dealt with wood shortage through conservation: able to have large state capacity.
177- British/Japanese/Chinese ecological policy may explain various different paths.
181- India still heavily forested till 19th century -> no need for coal -> no Indian words for coal even!
Summary: coal-response to deforestation in Europe, China failed to respond. Japan innovated ecologically, no deforestation crisis in India -> only Europe developed key coal-steam-iron complex.

## Chapter 7: Science and Technology in India, 1600-1800
189- Default assumption: static East, dynamic + curious West: pretty wrong.
191- No coherent "science" until later, only investigation + systematic observation: also present in India.
198- Significant exchange of ideas between Europe and India. In 17th and 18th centuries especially in Mysore.
210- Rapid incorporation of European technical knowledge and skills among Indian artisans.
218- Comparable diffusion of technical skills among Indian artisans ->
220- Big debate: Science -> Technology or Technology -> Science?
Summary: India on par with Europe in science/technology/artisans skill in 17th-18th centuries. Theory fails to explain divergence.

## Chapter 8: Modern Industry in Early 19th Century India
225- Externalist versus Internalist (Colonialism versus institutions/culture/endowments) - now recent Indian scholarship.
226- Cheap United Kingdom goods + elimination of courtly centers of demand harmed Indian artisans 1800-1850.
234- Indian shipbuilding industry destroyed. Heavy iron United Kingdom export tax on Indian ships/goods.

*   **Page 239**: Clear potential for Russiesque industrialization based on exported United Kingdom capital and expertise, severely hampered by Britain-first style policies (e.g., iron).
*   **Page 244**: European states promoted industry with national interest; the East India Company state promoted United Kingdom manufacturing interests, not Indian.
*   **Page 248**: State sponsored education and investigation led to increased industry, which was not present in the East India Company state.
*   **Summary**: United Kingdom colonial rule in India protected United Kingdom goods at Indian expense, failed to nurture Indian industry, did not invest in education, and destroyed political centers of demand and knowledge generation.
